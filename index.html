CHIEF COMPLAINT

The Problem

Presenting symptom: IV calculation errors under pressure

IV drip rate calculations are one of the most common tasks a nurse performs on shift. Changing a rate from 100mL over 60 minutes to 100mL over 90 minutes requires quick math under pressure, often while managing multiple patients at once. Errors here can directly harm patients.

Underlying condition: the unsafe question

Primary research with an ICU nurse (ongoing), plus informal input from her colleagues, surfaced a bigger and far less obvious problem. Nursing units are often hierarchical and quick to judge. Asking a basic clinical question, even one that could prevent harm, can carry real professional consequences including performance write-ups.

A nurse who asks "can you hang potassium for an IV push?" is asking a question with an obvious answer to experienced colleagues. Asking it out loud signals inexperience in a way that follows a nurse's reputation. So nurses don't ask. They guess. That puts patients at risk.

This insight reshaped the product, with the calculator becoming the entry point and the private clinical Q&A serving as the differentiated value.

HISTORY

Discovery & Validation

Primary research

  • Ongoing primary research with an ICU nurse, daily user, plus informal input from colleagues on real shifts
  • Found two distinct unmet needs: calculation speed under pressure, and access to clinical knowledge without social risk
  • Confirmed existing tools (paper, mental math, basic calculators, legacy desktop apps) had no natural language or voice input
  • Validated that nurses use personal phones on shift, which shaped the PWA decision over a native app

The insight that shaped the product

"I can't ask my charge nurse this without it going on my record."

That single sentence defined the product's core value: a private, judgment-free clinical companion, not just a faster calculator. The "Ask Anything" feature wasn't in the original concept. It came directly from this.

The mentor challenge

Why wouldn't a nurse just use ChatGPT?
A general chatbot can technically answer clinical questions. What it can't replicate is workflow integration and trust framing. ShiftMate lives where the work happens, connects the calculator and Q&A in one context, and is explicitly positioned as judgment-free for clinical questions. "No judgment here, ever" is doing real work in an environment where asking the wrong question has professional consequences. The technical moat deepens with the drug reference database — clinically sourced data a general AI can't replicate through positioning alone.

TREATMENT PLAN

The Solution

FEATURE 01

AI natural language IV calculator

Nurses type or speak naturally: "change room 4 dopamine from 100mL over 60 min to 90 min." The AI parses, finds the right line, updates the rate, confirms in plain English. No forms, no navigation.

FEATURE 02

Voice input with Web Speech API

One tap starts. A second tap stops and triggers the parse. Nurses are on their feet holding equipment, and a form is not a realistic interaction model on shift.

FEATURE 03

Multi-line shift tracker with CONT / BOLUS modes

A single toggle switches between continuous infusion and bolus. Continuous lines track bag replacement. Bolus lines count down to completion and auto-reset for the next dose. Different clinical contexts, different timer behavior.

FEATURE 04 — Beta feedback drove this

Bag replacement countdown

Surfaced in the first 24 hours of real shift use. Bag life calculates automatically from volume and rate with no extra input. Nurse taps "Hung now." Notifications fire at 10 and 5 minutes before the bag runs dry. The insight: nurses need to arrive before the bag empties, not after, to avoid waking the patient.

FEATURE 05 — Beta feedback drove this

Dose calculator with weight-based dosing

When a doctor orders "dopamine at 5 mcg/kg/min" the nurse needs to know what mL/hr to set on the pump. The dose calculator takes ordered dose, dose unit, patient weight (kg or lbs), and bag concentration, and outputs the pump rate. Supports ten common dosing units including mcg/kg/min, mg/kg/hr, and units/hr.

FEATURE 06

Private Clinical Q&A: Ask Anything

A floating button opens a full-screen confidential chat. The AI is prompted as a non-judgmental clinical assistant. Opening message: "No judgment here, ever." This addresses the real problem: not the math, the fear of asking.

FEATURE 07

iOS install prompt

On first visit from mobile Safari, a 3-step guide to home screen installation appears automatically. The app teaches itself. No instruction burden on the user.

Risks & Mitigations

Because ShiftMate provides clinical information, accuracy was prioritized over completeness. The product includes first-launch disclaimers, stores no PHI, and positions AI responses as informational support rather than medical direction. The drug reference database, when shipped, will use clinically-sourced data with source attribution. Background notifications on iOS are an honest limitation of the PWA model — documented and deferred to the native app phase.

Two revenue paths

  • Path A — B2C Nurse Subscription: Individual nurses pay a monthly fee. The freemium model proves value before the institutional sale. Benchmarked against comparable clinical reference tools already in market.
  • Path B — B2B Hospital License: Sell to hospital systems, not individual nurses. The value proposition to administrators is patient safety and liability reduction, not nurse productivity. Per-seat or per-facility pricing. This is the larger revenue path.

Scale path

  • 0 to 1K: Unit-level word of mouth — nurses talk, especially about tools that solve real shift problems
  • 1K to 10K: Hospital champion cultivation — identify advocates, treat them as co-builders, drive floor-by-floor adoption
  • 10K to 100K: Nursing school partnerships — 1,700 programs graduating 180K nurses per year. ShiftMate as the clinical rotation tool means every cohort enters the workforce already using it

Long-term value layer

The drug reference database, once built with clinically-sourced data, has standalone licensing value to EHR vendors and clinical decision support platforms. That's a separate but high-value revenue stream beyond the consumer and hospital paths.

PLAN OF CARE

Product Roadmap

Three phases from MVP validation through enterprise platform. Phases are driven by user signal, not arbitrary timelines.

PHASE 1 · COMPLETE

MVP — Calculation & Entry-Level AI

  • Voice-first IV rate calculator with natural language input
  • Multi-line shift tracker with CONT/BOLUS modes
  • Bag replacement countdown from volume and rate
  • Weight-based dose calculator: mcg/kg/min, mg/kg/hr, 10 dose units
  • Private AI clinical Q&A: Ask Anything
  • iOS install prompt and first-launch disclaimer
  • GA4 analytics with custom events from day one
  • Validation gate: confirm nurses find core value before building further
PHASE 2 · IN PLANNING

V2 — Clinical Intelligence Layer

  • Initial drug reference database: 60+ common IV medications with dosing ranges, high-alert flags, and incompatibility warnings
  • AI citation layer: every Q&A answer cites the source document or clinical reference. Nurses see where answers come from.
  • Document upload for nursing schools and small clinics. Institutions can add their own protocols and the AI learns from them
  • Native app via Capacitor: enables real background notifications when the phone is locked
  • Alert polish: background bag replacement and bolus notifications while the phone is locked
PHASE 3 · FUTURE

Enterprise — Hospital Platform

  • B2B hospital licensing: sell to systems, not individuals
  • Hospital policy-trained AI: connect the hospital's own protocols and within days the AI operates by their specific practices. No other tool offers this.
  • Full citation layer: every AI response cites the specific hospital policy or clinical source it draws from
  • SharePoint / intranet integration: connect ShiftMate to existing hospital infrastructure without fighting IT procurement
  • Institutional admin dashboard: usage analytics, compliance reporting, and seat management
  • HIPAA compliance and SOC 2 certification: required for hospital procurement
VITALS

Metrics & Beta

GA4 was instrumented before the first external user with custom events tracking every meaningful interaction. Data is clean from day one.

Custom events tracked

  • disclaimer_accepted: counts true unique new users
  • line_added (source: ai or manual): measures calculator adoption
  • ai_parse (method: voice or text, action: add or update): measures AI feature usage
  • chat_opened: measures Ask Anything adoption
  • timer_started (type: continuous or bolus): measures timer engagement
  • bag_started: measures bag replacement feature adoption

Primary signals being watched

  • Weekly active users: the primary retention signal for shift workers
  • Returning sessions: did a nurse come back on their next shift?
  • Voice vs text input ratio: informs mobile UX priority
  • Day-7 and Day-30 retention: the core PMF gate

What beta has already validated

  • Timer request surfaced within 24 hours of first real shift usage
  • Bag replacement countdown identified as the primary need over elapsed count-up
  • Unit selectors (mg, mcg, g) needed for non-volume drug entries
  • Dose calculator need confirmed by concentration question from active beta user
  • Layout feedback (5-column grid too cramped on vertical phone) addressed immediately
  • Voice parsing race condition identified and fixed before production push
DISCHARGE NOTES

What I Learned

DISCOVERY FIRST

The real problem is almost never the surface problem. The calculator was obvious. The unsafe question took a dinner conversation to find.

VELOCITY

Shipping something imperfect to real users teaches more than any amount of planning. Beta feedback in the first 24 hours reshaped the roadmap more than weeks of speculation would have.

SCOPE DISCIPLINE

Every feature that didn't ship was a decision, not a failure. The drug database was built and ready. Holding it back was right — beta asked for timers and dose calculators instead.

HONESTY

When a mentor challenged the ChatGPT objection, the right answer wasn't to deflect. Acknowledging what's thin and knowing what closes the gap is more credible than over-defending.

STAGING

Nurses are on real shifts with real patients. A staging branch before every production push isn't optional — it's the minimum bar for clinical tools.

PROXIMITY

The sharpest insight in this project came from one dinner conversation. You can't replicate that kind of access — and it's why the product is better than what a team building from the outside would have made.
